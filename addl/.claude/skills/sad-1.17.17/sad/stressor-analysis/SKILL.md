# stressor-analysis (S3)

Produces the Stressor Catalog fragment of a SAD. The catalog is a typed list of stressors generated by random simulation across six frameworks (PESTLE, Porter's 5 Forces, Business Model Canvas, abstraction-stressing, flow-stressing, boundary-stressing) plus the eight destruction questions. Each stressor has a Type (`Structural` / `Topological` / `Business` / `Combined`), a coherent narrative (cause -> propagation -> attractor outcome), and a contextual framing (not a technical event).

This is the most important sub-skill of the meta-skill. The empirical strength of the residual architecture comes from the breadth, diversity, and depth of the stressors generated here.

---

## What this sub-skill is really doing

Stressor analysis formalizes what senior architects do intuitively. When a senior architect asks "what if this changes?", "what if this stops being true?", "what if the entity ceases to exist?", "what if the flow changes completely?" -- that is stressor analysis. The sub-skill turns those questions into a structured, typed, traceable catalog.

Three orientations to internalize before starting:

1. **The criticality signal is architectural maturity.** When new stressors are increasingly survived by combinations of existing residues (Combined type), the architecture has reached the point where it absorbs things it was not designed for. This is the empirical signature of a mature architecture (cited O'Reilly L1812-1816). Senior architects produce this outcome without being able to formalize why. This sub-skill makes the production reproducible.

2. **"You cannot map hyperliminal systems."** (O'Reilly L2738). The goal of this sub-skill is NOT to model the business context perfectly. It is to generate enough stressor diversity that the architecture survives continuous transformation. A complete map is impossible; a critical (in the Kauffman sense) decomposition is achievable.

3. **All stressors must go in the list, no matter how ridiculous.** (O'Reilly L1763-1768, literal). What looks absurd today may be normal tomorrow. War, regulatory reversal, pandemic, mass-market substitute -- all welcome. Probability filtering is structurally banned (R-20).

---

## When to invoke

- The Flow Analysis fragment (S2 output) exists and has been approved.
- The user is ready for a multi-session catalog build (a thorough analysis takes hours, not minutes).
- Stakeholders from outside engineering are available (PESTLE and Porter draw heavily on non-engineering expertise).

## When NOT to invoke

- Flow Analysis fragment does not exist (run S2 first).
- The user is asking for "high-probability risks" or "the top 10 risks". This is risk management, not stressor analysis. Stressors are not risks; probability is not part of the definition (R-20). Redirect or refuse.
- The user wants a single-framework session (just PESTLE, just abstraction-stressing). The diversity of frameworks is the random simulation; a single framework rebuilds the bias the simulation is meant to defeat.
- The catalog already exists and the user wants edits without re-running the simulation. Edits to a closed catalog are handled by an explicit re-open + re-type pass; do not silently amend.

## Pre-conditions

- The **S2 gate is marked `[x] approved`** in the project gate tracker (`FLOW.md`), not merely produced -- verify before producing (root `SKILL.md` §Gate approval protocol).
- `business-view.md` exists and is approved.
- `naive-architecture.md` exists and is approved.
- `flow-analysis.md` exists and is approved.

S3 depends on all three because:

- Business View tells the simulator what the system is for (Porter and BMC need context).
- Naïve Architecture identifies the components the stressors will be tested against (Structural-typed stressors map to columns of the Contagion Matrix in S4).
- Flow Analysis lists the flows that flow-stressing will iterate over.

## Handoff contract

- **Consumes:** `business-view.md` (+ invariants -- the simulator's context for Porter/BMC), `naive-architecture.md` (the component list for the every-noun pass), `flow-analysis.md` (the flows that flow-stressing iterates over) -- prior gate S2 must be `[x] approved`.
- **Produces:** `stressor-catalog.md` (every stressor typed Structural / Topological / Business / Combined per R-13, with narratives).

## Workflow

### Step 1 -- Run the six frameworks

For each framework in `shared/stressor-frameworks.md`, generate stressors. Do not stop after one. Do not weight frameworks by perceived relevance. The recommended session sequence:

1. **PESTLE** (15-30 min) -- macro environment. Best with non-engineering stakeholders.
2. **Porter's 5 Forces** (15-30 min) -- competitive context.
3. **Business Model Canvas** (30-60 min) -- internal business mechanics.
4. **Abstraction stressing** (30-60 min) -- challenge every domain noun (drives Step 2). Include the **every-noun pass** (O'Reilly Rule 3), run **exhaustively, not as a sample**: EVERY noun in EVERY component's name, each against fails / multiplies / schema-changes / deleted-in-use / replayed (~5-7 stressors per component). A representative handful defeats the random-simulation purpose. Cheap; surfaces the highest-blast-radius stressors (shared Resources / Utilities). Do not pre-curate the output. See `shared/stressor-frameworks.md` §4.
5. **Flow stressing** (15 min per flow) -- attack each row of the Flow Analysis table.
6. **Boundary stressing** (15-30 min) -- stress co-location decisions; discovers operational Topological residues (scaling / failure-isolation / change-cadence / resource-profile / security-zone). Under R-25 the default is one Manager = one service (private Engines/RAs co-located); this lens resolves shared Engines/RAs and rare Manager co-locations.

A first pass typically produces 30-50 raw stressors of mixed quality. Quality filtering does NOT happen at generation time.

### Step 2 -- Apply the eight destruction questions

For each domain entity (in the Naïve Architecture), each flow (in the Flow Analysis), and each business assumption (in the Business View), run the eight questions. These overlap with abstraction stressing and flow stressing but are tighter prompts and surface stressors the broader frameworks miss:

| # | Question | What it surfaces |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | What if this **changes**? | Volatility in attributes, semantics, or rules attached to the entity / flow. |
| 2 | What if this **ceases to exist**? | Removal of an actor, a flow source, an external system, a regulation that defined it. |
| 3 | What if this **scales** by 10x or 100x? | Volume / load / cardinality shifts that break current assumptions. |
| 4 | What if this **fragments**? | Single entity becoming many (one Customer becoming a Fleet; one Charger becoming a Stall with many Plugs). |
| 5 | What if **regulation appears**? | New legal / compliance / sovereignty constraints. |
| 6 | What if its **behavior changes**? | The same entity acting differently than the current model assumes (a Customer that pre-pays vs post-pays; a Resource that goes from sync to async). |
| 7 | What if **ownership changes**? | The entity changes hands (a Fleet acquired; an Account inherited; data ownership shifting from us to a partner). |
| 8 | What if **temporality changes**? | The lifetime, duration, or sequence of the entity shifts (a session expected in minutes lasting 14 hours; a one-off event becoming periodic). |

Each question is a generator. Use them deliberately on the entities most central to the Naïve Architecture; thin output on a central entity is itself a signal that the entity is under-stressed in the catalog.

### Step 3 -- For each stressor, build a coherent narrative

A stressor is NOT a one-liner. O'Reilly L1484-1489 is explicit: "Stressors will be connected to a story that will reveal what actually happens in the context... using the initial event of a stressor and creating a narrative around it the journey is easier and may lead in unexpected directions than simply trying to describe or map the attractor."

A good stressor has:

- A **triggering event** (what happens to set off the chain).
- A **propagation chain** (how the event moves through the business / system).
- An **attractor outcome** (the new business state the system tends toward).

**Bad stressor (one-liner):**

> "PostgreSQL goes down."

**Good stressor (narrative):**

> "During a market volatility spike, the PostgreSQL replica falls behind primary by 15 minutes. The replay queue grows, consumers read stale market data, traders see inconsistent candles across screens, and the support inbox fills with complaints. The system has shifted into an attractor where the source of truth is contested and the brokerage's compliance posture is exposed."

The narrative version reveals the attractor and produces a meaningful residue. The one-liner version cannot drive a residue and risks being misclassified.

### Step 4 -- Verify each stressor is contextual, not technical

O'Reilly L1857-1862 is direct: "technical architects with heavy technical backgrounds will generate very technical lists of stressors. This is not a good sign!" The most common error in this sub-skill is generating stressors that name technologies instead of business consequences.

| Anti-pattern (too technical) | Reframe (contextual) |
|---|---|
| "Redis goes down" | "During an extreme market event the business needs real-time pricing for tens of thousands of concurrent traders; the assumption that an in-memory cache will hold the working set no longer holds." |
| "DNS poisoning" | "An attacker controls the route between charger and cloud; auth flows can be redirected silently to a mock cloud." |
| "DB failover takes 30 seconds" | "During a regulatory audit window the system must demonstrate transactional integrity within 5 seconds, but the underlying durability mechanism cannot guarantee that during failover." |
| "Kafka lag" | "An ingestion bottleneck during peak hours pushes the business into a delayed-trade attractor where reconciliation lags compliance by hours." |

When in doubt, ask: "what business outcome would this stressor produce if we made no architectural change?" If the answer is "the technology would log an error", the framing is too technical. If the answer is "the business would shift to an attractor where X is no longer true", the framing is contextual.

### Step 5 -- Type each stressor

For every stressor that survived the narrative + contextual reframe checks, assign exactly one type. Typing is mandatory (R-13). The typing rules:

| Type | Criterion |
|---|---|
| **Structural** | The stressor drives a change in IDesign components or call topology. Examples: new car models, server failure, billing errors, electricity failure. Feeds Contagion Matrix in S4. |
| **Topological** | The stressor drives a change in deployment topology (geography, tenancy, replication, latency zones). Examples: data-residency regulation, multi-region disaster recovery, jurisdiction-specific compliance. Feeds Topological Residue Map in S4. May also have component impact and additionally appear in Contagion Matrix marked `(T)`. |
| **Business** | The stressor produces a business decision but no software change. Examples: market crash (pivot the business), capacity planning (real-estate), competitor pricing pressure (margin strategy). Feeds Business Residues Log in S4. |
| **Combined** | The stressor is already survived by an existing combination of residues. No new component, no deployment change, no business decision. Recorded as Looping Signals in S4. This type usually appears late in the simulation as criticality approaches. |

When in doubt between `Business` and `Structural`, ask: does this require code change? If yes, Structural. If only policy / commercial / real-estate change, Business.

When in doubt between `Topological` and `Structural`, ask: does this change deployment unit boundaries? If yes, Topological (and additionally Structural if components also change).

### Step 6 -- Watch for edge cases that are actually broken abstractions

O'Reilly L1547-1562: "the presence of a lot of edge cases may indicate linear thinking replacing architecture work with its own devices... a sign that the base abstractions around entities and processes need to be stressed to find weaknesses".

When generating stressors, if a candidate keeps reading like a "minor edge case" of a current model, check whether it is actually evidence that the model is wrong. Typical tell: the candidate requires an `if`-branch in code that the architect resists adding because "it would muddy the abstraction".

When this happens, do NOT add the stressor as a low-impact `Structural`. Instead, re-run Step 2 on the entity whose abstraction is in tension and surface the deeper stressor (e.g., "this entity is not what we modeled it as"). The deeper stressor produces a residue; the surface edge case produces a patch.

### Step 7 -- Document the standard fields for each stressor

The catalog table has columns:

| Column | Required | Format |
|---|---|---|
| `#` | Yes | Sequential identifier (1, 2, 3, ...). |
| `Type` | Yes | `Structural` / `Topological` / `Business` / `Combined`. Mandatory per R-13. |
| `Stressor` | Yes | The stressor with narrative -- triggering event + propagation chain + attractor outcome. Not a one-liner. |
| `Detection` | Yes | How would the team learn this stressor has happened? (alarm, complaint, market scan, legal notice, etc.) |
| `Attractor` | Yes | The new business state the system tends toward under this stressor. One line, business framing. |
| `Business Reaction` | Yes | What the business does in this attractor (may be "n/a" for Combined or pure-Structural rows). |
| `Technical Change to Residue (IDesign)` | Yes for Structural / Topological with component impact; `(n/a)` for Business and Combined | The residue in IDesign vocabulary: which components are added / changed / split / merged. Use proper naming per R-06. **This is a candidate type, not a commitment.** S3 is lateral-mode (R-22); naming a residue `XEngine` here is a first guess. S5 may re-type it (e.g. an "Engine" that turns out to pass the cappuccino test becomes a Utility, or R-24 collapses it into an extended ResourceAccess). A re-type in S5 is NOT a traceability mismatch -- the residue # still traces; only its IDesign stereotype was refined. Do not over-commit the stereotype in S3. |

### Step 8 -- Stop condition

The simulation does NOT stop after a fixed time or a fixed stressor count. It stops when **looping** appears: new stressors are increasingly survived by existing residues (Combined-typed). This is the empirical signature that the catalog has reached useful breadth for the current iteration -- and it is the same signal that the architecture is approaching criticality (`shared/glossary.md` "Looping", O'Reilly L1812-1816).

**Looping is the measure of volume -- not a stressor count.** Never target a number. Two failure modes:

- **Looping has NOT appeared -> the catalog is under-sampled.** Keep generating: run the every-noun pass exhaustively (Step 1.4 -- it is the cheapest source of genuine volume), push the random/decision-tree harder, exercise frameworks not yet used, and rerun Step 2 on entities that produced thin output. Do NOT declare the catalog ready without looping just because it "feels like enough" -- that is the curse of high dimensionality (O'Reilly L660-669).
- **Looping HAS appeared -> stop.** More stressors past the looping point is redundancy, not criticality. Generating hundreds of near-duplicate stressors (e.g. the same race-condition shape on every Manager) past looping is wasted effort, not rigor. A catalog that loops at ~55 has sampled the attractor space; a peer pipeline at 182 (deduped from 241) over-generated past its own looping point.

If looping appears very early (e.g., the 3rd stressor is already Combined), the early stressors covered the attractor space well -- but verify this is real and not a sign of insufficient diversity in the early frameworks (re-check that PESTLE/Porter/BMC/abstraction/flow/boundary + the exhaustive every-noun pass all ran).

### Step 9 -- Final pass: hygiene checks

Before declaring the catalog ready:

| Check | Verification | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Every row has a Type | All Type cells non-empty and within the allowed values. | R-13 |
| Every row has a narrative | The Stressor column reads as a story (triggering event + propagation + attractor), not a one-liner. | This sub-skill, Step 3 |
| No technical-only stressors | No row names only a technology (Redis, Kafka, DNS, DB) without business framing. | This sub-skill, Step 4 |
| No probability column | No column header or annotation containing: probability, likelihood, risk, cost, complexity, effort, priority. | R-20 |
| At least one "ridiculous" stressor | The catalog has at least one stressor that would have been filtered by a probability column. War, pandemic, mass-market substitute, regulatory reversal, etc. | R-20 spirit |
| Multiple frameworks used | The catalog references at least 3 of the 6 frameworks (typically all 6 for a thorough simulation). | Stressor diversity |
| Each Structural row has a Technical Change | Every `Structural`-typed row has non-`(n/a)` content in the Technical Change column. | R-13 + R-18 (downstream) |
| Each Topological row has a deployment implication | Every `Topological`-typed row hints at the deployment boundary the residue will demand. | R-19 (downstream) |
| Each Business row has a rationale-of-record | Every `Business`-typed row has Detection + Attractor + Business Reaction so the Business Residues Log can be built from it in S4. | R-13 + guardrail #3 |
| Each Combined row references prior residues | Every `Combined`-typed row implicitly references the residues that survive the stressor; the explicit `Survived by combination of #X, #Y` trace is added by S4 in the Looping Signals table. | R-17 (downstream) |
| **Count and distribution reconciles (script-backed)** | After drafting and **before** parking the fragment at the gate, run the bundled `scripts/fragment-checks/check_counts.py <fragment>`. The per-Type counts in the "Count and distribution" prose MUST equal the Type-column tally; the script hard-fails on drift (this is the audit-iter-5 "9 Topological / 6 Combined vs table 8/7" class, caught at production instead of audit). | R-13 count-consistency (feedback F-02) |

If hygiene checks fail, do not write the fragment -- refuse (see §Refusal conditions). **Generalize the count check:** re-derive from the table EVERY aggregate number you recite in prose (per-Type distribution, totals) -- never hand-carry a count across an edit. `check_counts.py` is the deterministic backstop, not a substitute for getting it right at the source.

## Common pitfalls

These are the failure modes the sub-skill must catch, beyond the deterministic hygiene checks.

### Pitfall 1 -- Technical-not-contextual stressors

Symptom: stressors named for technology failures. "Redis goes down", "DB failover", "Kafka lag".

Why dangerous: the stressor maps to one specific technical fix and misses the business context that determines whether the fix matters. A residue against "Redis goes down" produces a Redis-redundancy decision; a residue against "the business needs real-time pricing during extreme events" produces a pricing-architecture decision that survives any in-memory-cache technology change.

Detection: every Stressor column entry that names only a technology vendor / product / mechanism without a business consequence chain.

Action: reframe per Step 4 table. If the architect cannot articulate the business consequence, the stressor is incomplete; remove it until the consequence is named.

### Pitfall 2 -- One-liner stressors without narrative

Symptom: Stressor column entries are 5-10 words. "Server failure." "Auth bypass." "Compliance change."

Why dangerous: a one-liner cannot reveal the attractor; without the attractor, the residue is undefined; S4 cannot read the matrix usefully.

Detection: word count + check for cause -> propagation -> attractor structure.

Action: rewrite per Step 3. If the architect cannot expand the one-liner into a narrative, the stressor is not yet a stressor -- it is a placeholder.

### Pitfall 3 -- Edge cases disguised as stressors

Symptom: candidate that reads like "minor variation of an existing scenario". The team is tempted to add a small `if` branch.

Why dangerous: per O'Reilly L1547-1562, this often signals that the underlying abstraction is broken. Adding the candidate as an edge case patches the abstraction; adding the deeper stressor as a real residue forces the abstraction to evolve.

Detection: candidate that the architect describes as "we just need to handle this one case" or "it's a small exception". A stronger signal: the proposed code shape is an `if`-cascade --

```
if isLateTick: ...
if isReplay: ...
if isDuplicate: ...
if isCorrection: ...
```

Each branch defends the original abstraction against a new domain reality. Per `shared/architectural-walking.md` §4.2: the if-cascade is the most visible linear-thinking anti-pattern. The lateral architect notices the second or third `if` and pauses to collapse the abstraction.

**Concrete examples of abstractions that collapse:**

- `Customer hasOne Car` -> fleet operators, shared ownership, rentals, autonomous vehicles. Not edge cases; the abstraction has shifted from a 1:1 relationship to a role-and-assignment model. (`architectural-walking.md` §4.5 Example A.)
- `Trade` -> synthetic trades, AI-generated liquidity, dark pools, fragmented venues. Not edge cases; the abstraction has shifted from a single atomic event to economic-event + reporting-fact + reconciliation-operation. (`architectural-walking.md` §4.5 Example B.)

Action: re-run Step 2 (eight destruction questions) on the entity in tension. The right stressor is usually one level deeper -- "the entity is not what we modeled" rather than "the entity has an unusual variant". Per R-22, lateral mode catches this; linear mode patches.

### Pitfall 4 -- Probability-driven filtering

Symptom: "we don't need this stressor, it won't happen". Or columns added for Likelihood / Priority.

Why dangerous: defeats the random simulation. Three of the most valuable EV charging residues (#3 broken key fob, #13 privacy regulation, #15 AFIR ten years later) would have been filtered out by a probability column.

Detection: column headers, conversational filtering by stakeholders.

Action: refuse per R-20. The catalog includes all generated stressors. Probability and cost re-enter later, in FMEA / ATAM (after the structural analysis is complete).

### Pitfall 5 -- Single-framework session

Symptom: the team runs PESTLE for 20 minutes and stops.

Why dangerous: each framework is biased by its lens. Their combination is the random simulation; a single framework rebuilds the bias.

Detection: catalog references only one of the six frameworks.

Action: continue. Run at least 3 of the 5 for a working catalog; all 5 for a thorough one.

## Typical residue shapes

When the architect derives a Technical Change to Residue (IDesign) from a Structural-typed stressor, the change is usually one of a small set of shapes. The catalog should not over-engineer; residues are typically small.

| Residue shape | Description | Example (EV charging) |
|---|---|---|
| **Decoupling** | Split a coupling that the stressor revealed. Two responsibilities that were one become two components. | Stressor #3 (failed login) decouples authentication from billing into `AuthEngine` + `BillingManager`. |
| **Buffer / queue** | Insert a queue between two components so one can tolerate the other's failure or backpressure. | A delay-tolerant flow between charger and cloud absorbs cellular outages. |
| **Idempotency** | Add an idempotency token to an operation so retries are safe. | Charge completion event idempotent across charger retries. |
| **Retry / replay** | Make a failed flow resumable by replay from a durable log. | Resumable charge session per matrix row 9b. |
| **Interface abstraction** | Replace a concrete dependency with an interface that hides the volatile thing. | `ChargerAccess` polymorphic over connector type for stressor #1 (new car models). |
| **Partitioning** | Split a single data plane along a stress axis (geographic, tenancy, customer segment). | Per-country `CustomerAccess` + CustomerDB for stressor #13 (privacy regulation). |
| **Sensor / detection** | Add a Resource and ResourceAccess to detect a previously-invisible event. | `SensorAccess` + `CameraAccess` for stressor #8 (damage). |
| **Degraded mode** | A component defines a contract for what it still does when its primary dependency is unavailable. | Charger holds cached credentials for offline unlock per stressor #9. |

When a residue does NOT fit one of these shapes, examine whether it is actually a rebuild (too large) or a feature addition (R-09 / R-10 violation). Small, contained changes are the norm; large rewrites driven by a single stressor usually indicate the stressor is too vague (collapse multiple stressors) or the existing architecture is already wrong.

## Refusal conditions

| # | Trigger | Rule | Returned message |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Any stressor lacks a Type. | R-13 | List the offending rows. Typing is mandatory; without it, S4 cannot route the stressor to the right sub-table. |
| 2 | The catalog contains a column named Probability, Likelihood, Risk Score, Cost, Complexity, Effort, or Priority. | R-20 + guardrail #6 | Remove the column. Probability and cost enter later, in FMEA / ATAM, not here. |
| 3 | The user explicitly requests that probability or cost be used to filter or sort stressors. | R-20 | Refuse the framing. Explain the random simulation rationale and the curse of high dimensionality. Offer to proceed without the filter. |
| 4 | The catalog has fewer than 10 stressors. | -- | Heuristic warning, not a hard refusal. Ask the user whether more frameworks should be run before closing the catalog. |
| 5 | The catalog uses only one framework. | Stressor diversity | Refuse. Single-framework catalogs are biased. Run at least 3 of the 5. |
| 6 | A Structural-typed stressor has `(n/a)` in the Technical Change column. | R-13 mistyping | The row is mistyped. If there is no technical change, the row is Business. Re-type. |

| 10 | A "stressor" is actually a requirement or a known feature request. | Definition | Refuse. A stressor is a fact about the context UNKNOWN to or unaccounted for in the naïve architecture. Feature requests are requirements; route them to PRD/SRD, not to the catalog. |
| 11 | A stressor names only a technology failure without business consequence (e.g., "Redis goes down"). | Pitfall 1, O'Reilly L1857-1862 | Reject. Reframe contextually per Step 4 table. If the architect cannot articulate the business consequence, remove the stressor until they can. |
| 12 | A stressor is a one-liner without narrative (cause -> propagation -> attractor missing). | Pitfall 2, O'Reilly L1484-1489 | Reject. Demand the narrative per Step 3. A one-liner cannot drive a residue. |
| 13 | A stressor reads as a "minor variant" or "small exception" of an existing scenario. | Pitfall 3, O'Reilly L1547-1562 | Flag as broken-abstraction candidate. Re-run Step 2 on the entity in tension. The deeper stressor (the abstraction is wrong) replaces the surface edge case. |

## How the six frameworks integrate

The frameworks are described in detail in `shared/stressor-frameworks.md`. This SKILL.md does NOT duplicate those descriptions; it sequences them and pairs them with the eight destruction questions (Step 2) which act as a deeper drill on entities surfaced by abstraction-stressing.

Key heuristic: when a framework produces few stressors, do NOT skip the framework. The thin output is itself information ("our business model is not heavily exposed to competitor pressure"). Note the thinness and proceed.

## Walks and frameworks are complementary -- the cognitive mode

The 6 frameworks above are the **generative method**. They produce structured, typed, traceable stressor catalogs that the downstream Contagion Matrix can consume. They are auditor-friendly outputs.

Architectural walking (`shared/architectural-walking.md`, mandated by R-22) is the **cognitive mode** that operates the method. Per O'Reilly L1190-1191 (literal): "This kind of walking is constantly stressing the current structure that the architect has built up." Walks and stressors are two framings of the same activity -- walk = perspective, stressor = artifact. Neither alone produces a good catalog:

| Framing alone | Failure mode |
|---|---|
| Frameworks without walks (linear mode -- R-22 violation) | Checklist mentality. Run PESTLE, produce one-liner stressors, declare done. The catalog passes hygiene but the Ri test will produce a value close to zero. |
| Walks without frameworks (pure lateral mode) | Unstructured intuition. The architect feels the domain is risky but cannot produce a typed catalog. The Contagion Matrix downstream is unbuildable. |

The 8 destruction questions (Step 2) are walks on entities. **Read them as walks, not as a checklist.** Inhabit each question. Imagine the world after the change. Follow the propagation chain. The narratives that result are the difference between a thin catalog and a useful one.

A linear-mode architect can satisfy every refusal condition mechanically and still produce a thin catalog. R-22 makes this risk explicit: the auditor cannot check cognitive mode, but the auditor will see its consequences in the Ri test (S6). If you are running this sub-skill and feel like a checker, you are in linear mode -- pause and read `shared/architectural-walking.md` before continuing.

## Why these rules

- **R-13.** Typing is the routing key for the entire downstream chain (S4). An untyped stressor is unroutable.
- **R-20.** Probability filtering destroys the random simulation. The whole method works because the architect explores attractor space, not likelihood space.
- **R-09.** Speculative design is the OPPOSITE of stressor analysis. Stressor analysis does NOT add components for hypothetical futures; it adds components because a stressor (random or systematic) revealed an attractor the architecture cannot survive.
- **R-10.** Solutions masquerading as requirements often hide here too. If a stakeholder offers a "stressor" that is really a feature they want, the framing is wrong. Reframe.
- **Narrative requirement (Step 3, O'Reilly L1484-1489).** Stressors without narrative cannot drive residues. The narrative IS the path from the triggering event to the attractor -- without it, the attractor is undefined.
- **Contextual not technical (Step 4, O'Reilly L1857-1862).** Technical-framed stressors map to technical fixes that survive at most one technology generation. Contextually-framed stressors map to residues that survive any technology generation that satisfies the same business need.
- **Edge case = broken abstraction (Step 6, O'Reilly L1547-1562).** Patching edge cases preserves a wrong abstraction; stressing the entity exposes the abstraction's failure and forces the right residue.
- **Stop condition via looping (Step 8, O'Reilly L1812-1816).** Stopping based on time or count, without looping, leaves the analysis incomplete. Looping is the empirical signature of approaching criticality.
